Normalizing Flows

Lecture: L18 • Prince: Chapter 16

Motivation

Unlike VAEs, normalizing flows give us exact likelihoods and exact sampling. No ELBO approximation needed.

  • Sample $\mathbf{x}$ from a complex distribution
  • Evaluate $\log \Pr(\mathbf{x})$ exactly
  • Key requirement: every layer must be invertible

1D Flow Intuition

Sampling Direction ($z \to x$)

Start from a simple base density $\Pr(\mathbf{z})$ (e.g., Gaussian). Map through an invertible function. Stretching lowers density; compression raises density.

Likelihood Direction ($x \to z$)

Apply the inverse map. A derivative (Jacobian) term corrects for local stretching/compression.

Change of Variables Formula

1D Case

$$\Pr(x) = \Pr(z) \cdot \left|\frac{dz}{dx}\right| = \Pr(f^{-1}(x)) \cdot \left|\frac{df^{-1}}{dx}\right|$$

D-Dimensional Case

$$\Pr(\mathbf{x}) = \Pr(\mathbf{z}) \cdot \left|\det \frac{\partial \mathbf{z}}{\partial \mathbf{x}}\right| = \Pr(f^{-1}(\mathbf{x})) \cdot \left|\det \mathbf{J}_{f^{-1}}(\mathbf{x})\right|$$

The absolute determinant of the Jacobian measures the local volume change.

Composition of Layers

A flow is a composition of $K$ invertible (bijective) layers:

$$\mathbf{x} = f_K \circ f_{K-1} \circ \cdots \circ f_1(\mathbf{z})$$

By the chain rule, the Jacobian factorizes:

$$\log \Pr(\mathbf{x}) = \log \Pr(\mathbf{z}) + \sum_{k=1}^{K} \log \left|\det \mathbf{J}_{f_k^{-1}}\right|$$

Training

Maximize the exact log-likelihood over the training data:

$$\mathcal{L} = \sum_{i} \log \Pr(\mathbf{x}_i) = \sum_{i} \left[\log \Pr(\mathbf{z}_i) + \sum_{k} \log \left|\det \mathbf{J}_k\right|\right]$$

Layer Design Desiderata

  1. Expressive enough for complex densities
  2. Bijective (one-to-one mapping)
  3. Fast inverse computation
  4. Fast Jacobian determinant computation

Layer Types

Linear Flows

$f(\mathbf{z}) = \boldsymbol{\Omega}\mathbf{z} + \mathbf{b}$ with invertible $\boldsymbol{\Omega}$. Naively $O(D^3)$ for determinant; LU factorization brings it to $O(D^2)$.

Elementwise Flows

Per-dimension invertible transform (no mixing between dimensions). Jacobian is diagonal ⇒ log-det is just a sum. Need permutations or coupling layers to mix dimensions.

Coupling Flows (RealNVP)

Split input into two groups $(\mathbf{z}_A, \mathbf{z}_B)$:

  • $\mathbf{x}_A = \mathbf{z}_A$ (identity)
  • $\mathbf{x}_B = g(\mathbf{z}_B; \theta(\mathbf{z}_A))$ (transformed conditioned on $\mathbf{z}_A$)

The Jacobian is block-triangular ⇒ determinant is the product of diagonal blocks (cheap!). Affine coupling: $g(z) = s \cdot z + t$ where $s, t$ are outputs of a neural network applied to $\mathbf{z}_A$.

Stack multiple coupling layers with alternating splits + permutations/1×1 convolutions between them to ensure all dimensions interact.

Autoregressive Flows (MAF/IAF)

Dimension $d$ is conditioned on earlier dimensions $1, \ldots, d-1$. The Jacobian is triangular ⇒ determinant is the product of diagonal entries. Masked design enables parallel computation in one direction.
